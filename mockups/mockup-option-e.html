Your Options

Understand your visa options

H-1B — Specialty Occupation
For professionals in fields that require at least a bachelor's degree. Employer-sponsored, subject to annual lottery.
O-1 — Extraordinary Ability
For individuals with extraordinary achievement in sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics. No annual cap.
L-1 — Intracompany Transfer
Allows multinational companies to transfer employees in managerial, executive, or specialized knowledge roles.
EB-1 — Priority Workers
Employment-based green card for persons with extraordinary ability, outstanding professors, or multinational managers.
EB-2 — Advanced Degree
Green card for professionals holding advanced degrees or those with exceptional ability. Includes the NIW self-petition option.
EB-3 — Skilled Workers
For skilled workers, professionals, and unskilled workers whose positions cannot be filled by qualified US workers.
F-1 — Student Visa
For full-time students at accredited US institutions. Includes OPT and STEM OPT extensions for post-graduation work.
TN — USMCA Professionals
Available to Canadian and Mexican citizens in qualifying professions under the USMCA trade agreement.

H-1B Specialty Occupation Visa

Duration: 3 years (extendable to 6+) · Employer-sponsored · Annual cap: 65,000 + 20,000 · Dual intent allowed

The H-1B visa is the most common path for foreign professionals to work in the United States. It requires a US employer to sponsor you for a position in a "specialty occupation" — a role that typically demands at least a bachelor's degree or its equivalent in a specific field. Every spring, USCIS conducts a lottery for the following fiscal year's cap-subject H-1B petitions, making early preparation essential.

Eligibility Requirements
  • ✓You hold a bachelor's degree (or higher) in the relevant specialty field, or its equivalent
  • ✓The position qualifies as a "specialty occupation" requiring theoretical or practical application of highly specialized knowledge
  • ✓A US employer is willing to sponsor and file a petition on your behalf
  • ✓The employer obtains a certified Labor Condition Application (LCA) from the Department of Labor
  • ✓You will be paid at least the prevailing wage for the occupation in the area of intended employment
Application Process
  1. 1
    Employer files Labor Condition Application (LCA)
    Your sponsoring employer submits an LCA to the Department of Labor, attesting to prevailing wage compliance and working conditions. Processing typically takes 7–10 business days.
  2. 2
    Register for the H-1B lottery (March)
    Between March 1–20 each year, employers submit electronic registrations. USCIS then conducts a random selection. In FY 2025, approximately 470,342 registrations were submitted for 85,000 available slots.
  3. 3
    Receive lottery selection notice
    Selected registrants are notified by late March. Your employer then has 90 days to file the full H-1B petition with USCIS, targeting an October 1 start date.
  4. 4
    Employer files Form I-129 petition
    The full petition includes your credentials, job description, LCA, and supporting documentation. Premium processing (15 business days) is available for an additional fee of $2,805.
  5. 5
    USCIS adjudicates the petition
    USCIS reviews and either approves, issues a Request for Evidence (RFE), or denies. Standard processing can take 3–6 months. An approval notice (I-797) is issued upon approval.
  6. 6
    Visa stamp and entry (if outside the US)
    If you are abroad, schedule a consular interview at a US embassy or consulate to obtain the H-1B visa stamp. If already in the US in valid status, you can begin work on October 1.
Frequently Asked Questions
What happens if I'm not selected in the lottery? +−
If your registration is not selected, you are not prohibited from registering again the following year. In the meantime, you may explore cap-exempt options (universities, nonprofit research organizations), or alternative visa categories such as O-1, L-1, or TN if you qualify.
Can I change employers on H-1B? +−
Yes. Under H-1B portability (AC21), you can begin working for a new employer as soon as they file an H-1B transfer petition on your behalf — you do not have to wait for USCIS approval, provided your prior H-1B was previously approved and you are maintaining valid status.
Does H-1B allow me to apply for a green card? +−
Yes. The H-1B is a "dual intent" visa, meaning you can simultaneously hold immigrant intent (i.e., plan to pursue a green card) without jeopardizing your H-1B status. Your employer can file an I-140 immigrant petition on your behalf while you are in H-1B status.
What is the H-1B cap exemption? +−
Certain employers are exempt from the annual H-1B cap: institutions of higher education, affiliated nonprofit entities, nonprofit research organizations, and government research organizations. Employees working at or for these institutions can be sponsored year-round without going through the lottery.
